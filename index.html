Foundation Ages 6–9 ›

FMS Status

Locomotion: Running and jumping emerging, landing control inconsistent, hopping just developing, lateral movement uncoordinated.

Object control: Two-handed catching and rolling competent, overarm throw developing (arm-dominant at 6–7, hip rotation emerging at 8–9), striking basic.

Body management: Static balance developing (10s eyes open target by age 7), rotation uncontrolled, posture holding brief only, body awareness limited.

Golf Movement Unlocked

  • Basic address position awareness — feel of weight distribution, feet width, spine tilt
  • Simple weight shift feel — heavy back, heavy front
  • Full swing pattern at reduced speed — shape exposure, not precision
  • Rotational throw rehearsal of downswing sequence

Cannot Yet Demand

  • Consistent posture holding through a swing — postural endurance not there
  • Deliberate weight transfer sequencing — body awareness still forming
  • Hip–shoulder separation — physically impossible at this stage
  • Any ground force concept — produces confusion or compensatory lunge
  • Speed or power intent — encodes arm dominance and early rotation

Fault Risk if FMS Gap Present

Lateral Sway: Most common fault at Foundation stage. Glute medius weak, proprioception immature — the weight transfer feel cue produces a lateral slide. Balance games are the intervention, not more swing instruction.
Reverse Pivot: When weight transfer feel is absent, juniors often shift lead-to-trail on the backswing. Correct with external focus (feel the pressure in your back foot at the top).
Early Rotation (speed-induced): When trying to swing at full effort the body outruns the arms — the braking system does not exist yet. Reducing speed cleans the pattern immediately.
High swing-to-swing variance: High attack angle SD often traces back to proprioceptive development gaps at this stage. Balance work now reduces variance measurably at the next stage.

Priority Activities

  • Full balance games library — all games, every session
  • 30-minute athletic skills session — throwing and catching
  • Flamingo freeze benchmark tracked each session
  • Catch-and-freeze observation — arms in front or behind at freeze
  • Dominant arm throwing distance recorded each session
  • Equal time on non-dominant arm in every throwing drill — non-negotiable
Awareness Ages 10–12 ›

FMS Status

Locomotion: Running mechanics maturing, hop and stick competent on dominant side, lateral shuffle controlled, jumping and landing improving — single-leg landing still variable.

Object control: Overarm throw showing hip-before-arm sequence (dominant side) — golf connection can now be named explicitly. One-handed catching reliable. Bilateral throwing emerging.

Body management: Static balance solid eyes open (20s+ target). Eyes-closed balance developing. Thoracic rotation beginning to separate from hips. Posture holding 15–20s achievable.

Golf Movement Unlocked

  • Weight transfer with awareness cues — shift-then-rotate concept accessible
  • Rotational throw explicitly connected to downswing — “this throw is your downswing” lands
  • Slow-motion swing sequencing with verbal countdown (1-backswing, 2-transition, 3-delivery, 4-through)
  • Threshold speed work beginning (speed ramp Weeks 1 and 2 from age 11–12)
  • Basic ground force language — load before rotate, plant and rotate
  • Hip–shoulder separation as a feel and a goal — not yet reliable under full speed

Cannot Yet Demand

  • Deliberate GRF application — glute bridge and med ball program from age 12 minimum, 13 recommended
  • Full X-factor under swing speed — breaks down under speed and fatigue
  • Consistent single-leg stability under load — SL RDL belongs in Loading stage
  • Thoracic independence under swing speed — available statically, not dynamically
  • Automatic sequencing — everything must be conscious and slow before fast

Fault Risk if FMS Gap Present

Early Rotation (now physical and sequencing): Tests 3, 5, and 6 are key. A 12-year-old scoring 0 on Test 3 has a braking deficit that will express as Early Rotation regardless of how much technical work is done on downswing sequencing.
Over the Top / Casting: Casting is a rotational sequencing failure — athletic root is an underdeveloped overarm throw pattern. Sideways rotational throw drills are the intervention, not swing drills.
Loss of Posture: If postural endurance is insufficient to hold address for 20 seconds statically (Test 4), it will not hold through a swing dynamically.
Flat Shoulder Plane: Often has a thoracic restriction root identifiable at Awareness stage on Test 6. T-spine mobility work is mandatory before any shoulder plane instruction.

Priority Activities

  • Throwing and catching ages 10–12 drills — especially catch-and-freeze and rotational throw both directions
  • Slow-motion swing with verbal countdown — from the first session it is appropriate
  • Resistance band sequencing station from age 11+
  • Hip IR mobility work for any junior flagging Test 5
  • Thoracic rotation in golf posture added to every warm-up for any junior flagging Test 6
  • Non-dominant arm bilateral emphasis remains non-negotiable
Loading Ages 13–15 ›

FMS Status

Locomotion: Hop and stick competent both sides, lateral bound developing, landing mechanics improving — growth-related instability possible. Assess individually.

Object control: Overarm throw hip-before-arm both sides. Rotational throw competent dominant side. Med ball introduction appropriate (2kg). Deceleration catch-and-resist trainable.

Body management: Static balance solid eyes closed (15–20s target). Thoracic rotation separating cleanly from hips on screen. Single-leg stability under eccentric load developing. Glute bridge and SL RDL now appropriate.

Puberty note: The Loading stage is the most physically complex window in junior development. Growth velocity varies dramatically between juniors of the same age — coordination can regress as limb lengths change faster than the nervous system adapts. Assessment must be individual at this stage. Age is a guide, not a rule.

Golf Movement Unlocked

  • Full swing sequencing concept — shift-then-rotate trainable with physical load behind it
  • Ground force load-before-rotate — language and physical capacity now aligned
  • Hip–shoulder separation training — X-factor as a physical capacity, not just a concept
  • Med ball deceleration session — full program appropriate from age 13
  • Speed ramp protocol — full four-week protocol appropriate
  • Glute bridge and hip load progression — foundational eccentric braking program

Cannot Yet Demand

  • Full GRF expression — loading capacity is under construction
  • SuperSpeed and Mach 3 — Stage 4 tools. Loading stage junior in speed clinic reinforces early rotation
  • Vertical force concepts — appropriate language is load before rotate and plant and rotate
  • Automatic sequencing at full speed — still requires conscious management
  • Loaded SL stability without growth assessment — growth-related instability makes individual assessment mandatory

Fault Risk if FMS Gap Present

Early Rotation (now a capacity and braking deficit): Early Rotation that has not responded to sequencing drills is a physical capacity problem. Glute bridge program and med ball deceleration session are the primary interventions — not more swing instruction.
Early Extension: Single-leg stability deficit is the primary physical root. A junior who cannot hold a stable SL RDL with a 3-second eccentric cannot manage the lead-side posting demand through impact.
Hanging Back: Trail side load failure. Often a glute and hip stability deficit on the trail side — not mental commitment but physical inability to transfer.
Speed clinic flag: Any Loading stage junior showing Early Rotation on intake screen who is placed in SuperSpeed or Mach 3 will reinforce early rotation. Screen every junior before speed clinic placement.

Priority Activities

  • Glute bridge progression (4-week program) as the foundational physical intervention
  • Med ball deceleration session from age 13
  • Full speed ramp protocol from early Loading stage
  • Foot pressure drill and resistance band sequencing station
  • Post-bridge hip development pathway from Week 5
  • Individual growth monitoring — reassess SL stability after any significant growth period
Expression Ages 16+ ›

FMS Status

Locomotion: Full locomotion competency expected. Loaded jumping, bounding, and lateral work all trainable.

Object control: Full rotational throw both sides. Med ball 3–5kg appropriate. Deceleration catch-and-resist at load. Bilateral control mature.

Body management: Static and dynamic balance mature. Thoracic independence solid. Postural endurance under full swing load trainable. SL stability under eccentric load — SL RDL, hip thrust, lateral bound to stick — is the primary training tool.

Golf Movement Unlocked

  • Everything. Full GRF expression and vertical force concept
  • SuperSpeed Protocol 1 and Mach 3
  • Full X-factor training under swing speed
  • Maximum intent swing work
  • HP 12-week ground force block — all four phases

Cannot Yet Demand

Nothing is withheld on FMS developmental grounds at Expression stage. Every limitation at this stage is diagnosable through screening. Expression stage is not a guarantee of readiness — it is the stage where full readiness is achievable.

Fault Risk if FMS Gap Present

At Expression stage, fault risk is almost entirely capacity debt from earlier stages. Faults managed with technique workarounds in Awareness and Loading will re-emerge under the increased load and speed of Expression stage training.
Early Rotation (capacity debt): A 16-year-old still showing Early Rotation under full swing speed has a braking capacity deficit not resolved in Loading stage. Realistic expectation: 8–12 weeks of dedicated physical work before full swing pattern integrity at maximum speed.
All other faults at Expression stage reflect either identifiable physical restrictions or technique faults with physical roots. The intake screen remains the starting point.

Priority Activities

  • HP 12-week ground force block for any junior in a performance pathway
  • Full speed ramp protocol with Trackman threshold tracking
  • Post-bridge hip development pathway Phases 3 and 4
  • SuperSpeed Protocol 1 from Week 5 of HP block
  • Mach 3 from Phase 3
  • Trackman data as primary outcome measure — path variance, attack angle SD, and smash factor tracked weekly

📋 JDP Physical Intake Screen

7 tests — 15 minutes — zero equipment cost beyond two tennis balls and a club. Run at the start of every term. Determines which of three pathways each junior follows.
Score rangePathwayProgram direction
12–14Pathway APhysical foundation solid — proceed with swing development
8–11Pathway BGaps present — parallel physical and swing program
0–7Pathway CSignificant deficits — athletic foundation prioritised for full term
Critical flag rule: Any single test scoring 0 requires the physical intervention for that test regardless of total score. A 13/14 with a 0 on hip IR is still an Early Rotation risk.
Test 1 — Single-Leg Balance, Eyes Open 90 sec

Stand on dominant leg, arms folded, eyes open. Time to foot touch, max 30s. Repeat non-dominant. Observe hip drop (Trendelenburg sign).

Score 2: 20+ sec both legs, pelvis level Score 1: 10–20s or side difference, mild hip drop Score 0: under 10s either leg, or clear hip drop

Swing fault if 0: Lateral pelvic sway, trail hip sinking at top, lead side collapse at impact. Cannot execute weight transfer with stability.

Test 2 — Single-Leg Balance, Eyes Closed 60 sec

Same leg, eyes closed. Time to foot touch, max 20s. Key diagnostic: drop from eyes-open to eyes-closed time reveals visual compensation reliance.

Score 2: 10+ seconds Score 1: 5–10 seconds Score 0: under 5s — heavy visual compensation, high Trackman variance

Golf implication: Eyes are on the ball during the swing — proprioceptive system must work independently of visual input.

Test 3 — Lateral Hop and Stick 90 sec

Hop laterally off two feet, land on one foot, stick 2 seconds. 3 attempts each side. Observe: knee tracking over foot, hip level, no trunk collapse.

Score 2: clean 2s stick both sides, good landing quality Score 1: stick achieved with wobble, or one side worse Score 0: cannot stick either side, knee collapses, hip drops

Swing fault if 0: Cannot decelerate lateral forces — spins off lead side through impact. Early Rotation highly probable. Physical program mandatory before swing work.

Test 4 — Golf Posture Hold, 20 Seconds 60 sec

Junior takes address position, holds 20s without shifting weight or losing spine angle. Observe from behind (pelvis level, weight even) and from side (spine angle maintained).

Score 2: 20s stable, pelvis level, spine angle held Score 1: hold achieved but visible shifting or early fatigue Score 0: cannot hold 20s or clearly asymmetric

Golf implication: Cannot maintain posture through a swing if they cannot hold it statically. Any fault involving early extension or loss of posture has roots here.

Test 5 — Hip Internal Rotation, Seated 60 sec

Sit on bench edge, knees 90°, feet hanging. Swing foot outward (creates hip IR). Both sides. Lead hip IR is the critical measurement for Early Rotation risk.

Score 2: generous range both sides, foot well past midline Score 1: limited range or one side noticeably tighter Score 0: very limited range or discomfort reported

Direct physical driver of Early Rotation: If lead hip cannot internally rotate adequately, pelvis spins open rather than shift-and-rotate in transition. This is a mobility problem, not a technique problem.

Test 6 — Thoracic Rotation in Golf Posture 60 sec

Club across shoulders. Golf posture. Rotate shoulders as far as possible keeping hips still. Observe from behind: do hips move with shoulders or stay independent?

Score 2: clear separation visible, shoulders well past hips Score 1: some separation but limited or one side restricted Score 0: hips and shoulders rotate as one block — no independent T-spine

Golf implication: Score 0 means cannot physically create X-factor regardless of coaching. T-spine mobility work is mandatory before sequencing drills.

Test 7 — Rotational Throw, Both Arms 90 sec

Side-on to wall 4m away. Three throws dominant, three non-dominant. Observe hip-before-arm sequence, lead foot plant, finish stability.

Score 2: clear hip-before-arm dominant side, non-dominant uses some rotation Score 1: some rotation but arm-dominant, non-dominant limited Score 0: pure arm throw both sides, no hip rotation

Note: Age 6–9 scoring 0 or 1 is normal — this is what athletic sessions are building. Age 12+ scoring 0 with 2+ years of golf is a significant athletic development gap.

Screen Administration — Groups of 8–12

Station rotation: 3–4 juniors per station, rotate every 4 minutes.

  • Station 1: Tests 1 and 2 — both balance tests, same spot
  • Station 2: Tests 3 and 4 — hop and stick, then posture hold
  • Station 3: Tests 5 and 6 — hip IR and thoracic rotation (coach must observe here)
  • Station 4: Test 7 — rotational throw against wall

Prep needed: printed scorecards, pen, two tennis balls, one club per junior, stopwatch

Pathway Programs

Pathway A — Score 12–14

Swing instruction with full technical intent. Physical maintenance: 5 min balance games in every warm-up. Age 10+ begin glute bridge program. Age 13+ begin ground force program.

Pathway B — Score 8–11

Swing development continues at reduced complexity. Target the specific 0/1 scores:

  • Tests 1–2 low: 10 min balance games every session, weekly flamingo freeze benchmark
  • Test 5 low: hip mobility in warm-up daily, 90/90 stretching, hip CARs
  • Test 6 low: thoracic rotation in golf posture added to every session
  • Test 7 low: athletic skills sessions replace or supplement technical swing sessions
  • Re-screen at 6 weeks
Pathway C — Score 0–7

Session structure: 50% athletic games, 30% short game, 20% full swing basics. Balance games every session. Throwing and catching session weekly. Home program: teeth-brushing single-leg balance. Re-screen at 6 weeks and end of term.

Tracking Screen Data Over Time

  • Run same 7-test screen every term — score trajectory confirms whether physical program is working
  • Track individual test scores not just total — identifies which system is improving and which is stalled
  • Cross-reference with Trackman for age 12+: as screening scores rise, attack angle variance should reduce
  • HP pathway: screen scores become part of development record alongside Trackman data

⚋ Balance Games Library

Core principle: Balance games must have an external goal the child is chasing. Balance is the vehicle, never the destination.

Levels: Foundation (6–7) — both feet available, slow movements. Building (7–8) — single leg introduced, external challenge. Challenge (8–9) — single leg under load, eyes closed, unpredictable challenge.

Game 1 — Flamingo Freeze (Foundation)

Call “flamingo” — every child lifts one foot and holds as long as possible. Three rounds, try to beat personal best each round. Record both legs separately — significant difference between sides is a development flag.

Progression ladder (one step per 2 weeks): arms out eyes open → arms crossed eyes open → catch/throw a ball while balancing → eyes closed arms out → eyes closed arms crossed → eyes closed on folded mat

Age 6–7 target: 10s eyes open. Under 5s = proprioceptive development priority. Coach observation: hip drop = weak glute medius — same deficit that causes lateral pelvic sway in golf.

Game 2 — Sumo Statue (Foundation/Building)

Partners on one leg, hands up, gently push partner’s hands. First to put foot down loses. Switch legs after each point — equal time both legs mandatory.

Trains reactive stability — absorbing external force while balancing on one leg. Same system that manages GRF during weight transfer. Hands only, gentle pushes — establish this rule immediately.

Game 3 — Balance and Throw (Building — most golf-specific)

Stand on one leg facing a target (hoop, bucket, chalk circle). Throw at target while maintaining balance. 3 throws per leg. Score: 1pt for hitting target, bonus point if stayed balanced all 3 throws without touching down.

Surface progressions: flat floor → folded mat (4cm) → folded mat eyes closed → Bosu/wobble board (age 9+). Spend more time on lead leg — maps directly to impact stability. Most directly golf-relevant game in the library — trains lead post-up and rotational throw simultaneously.

Game 4 — Hopscotch Target (Building)

Classic hopscotch but must stick each single-foot landing for 1 second before next hop. Golf version: grid ends with single-leg “finish position” — arms out, balanced like a golf follow-through.

Teaches finish position as a balance challenge not swing instruction. Juniors who practise this show better finish positions when they swing a club.

Game 5 — Stepping Stones (Building)

Flat markers in irregular pattern across 5m. Cross using markers only, one foot per marker. Time it.

Progressions: carry a ball across without dropping → cross without same foot on two consecutive markers → carry and throw to target from a marker mid-crossing

Game 6 — Eyes-Closed Single-Leg (Challenge)

Stand on one leg, eyes closed, hold as long as possible. Count seconds out loud. Age 8–9 target: 10 seconds each leg.

Home challenge: Teeth-brushing morning and night — 2 minutes of proprioceptive training in an existing routine. No equipment. Isolates proprioceptive system completely — during the swing eyes are on the ball, proprioception manages body position.

Game 7 — Shark Attack (Challenge — best finish-session game)

Flat markers scattered, one fewer than number of children. Children move freely. Call “shark” — everyone must get on a marker and balance on one leg. Child without a marker = shark next round. Remove one more marker each round.

Combines locomotion, reaction, single-leg balance under time pressure, and competitive stakes. Balance under time pressure after sprinting = more neurologically valuable than controlled static balance.

Suggested 8-Minute Balance Block

  • 0–2 min: Flamingo freeze — establish baseline, record times both legs
  • 2–4 min: Balance and throw — single leg targeting, both legs and both arms
  • 4–6 min: Hopscotch target with golf finish position
  • 6–8 min: Shark attack — finish on maximum energy

🏍 Throwing & Catching

Throwing and catching at ages 6–12 trains three things that transfer directly to golf: rotational sequencing (body learning to fire segments in order), deceleration and catching (the braking system), and hand-eye coordination under movement. None needs to be framed as golf. Just athletic, fun, progressively challenging.
Ages 6–7 Foundation movers ›

Bilateral development is the priority. Both hands equally. Fun over form. No technique correction — let the goal organise the movement. Short bursts, 5 minutes maximum per drill.

Drill 1 — Two-Handed Chest Pass and Catch

Soft ball, 2–3m apart, throw from chest, catch with both hands pulling ball into body. Progress: increase distance, vary ball speed.

Golf transfer: Pulling-into-body catch = neurological ancestor of the pulling chain (lats, rhomboids) that controls club through impact.

Drill 2 — Underarm Rolling and Field

Roll ball to partner who fields and returns. Dominant hand, non-dominant, then both. Progress: increase speed, vary direction, add lateral movement. Do not correct getting low to field — just reward stopping the ball.

Drill 3 — Overarm Throw at a Target (most important drill at this age)

Soft ball, target on wall/fence. Throw dominant arm first — no correction of throwing technique. Then non-dominant.

Golf transfer: Overarm throw is the single closest movement to the golf downswing sequence available at this age — hip opens before shoulder, shoulder before arm, arm before hand, hand before ball — identical kinematic sequence to the golf downswing. Every throw unconsciously rehearses golf sequencing. Do not correct throwing technique at 6–7.

Drill 4 — Balloon Keep-Up

Keep balloon in air using alternating hands only. Progress: headers only, then alternate hand and head, count streaks, partner volley.

Golf transfer: Tracking unpredictable moving object and timing a strike = foundational hand-eye demand of golf, slowed down to a speed a 6–7 nervous system can encode.

Drill 5 — Throw and Sprint to Catch Own Rebound

Throw ball at wall at angle. Sprint to catch rebound before it bounces twice. Combines throwing (rotational sequencing), tracking (hand-eye), and locomotion in one high-engagement activity.

Ages 8–9 Pattern builders ›

Directional throwing introduced. Single-arm catching. Rotational awareness can be shaped now.

Drill 1 — Sideways Throw (most golf-specific at this age)

Stand side-on to wall or partner. Load trail hip back, throw — hip opens before arm releases. Both arms: dominant then non-dominant. Progress: increase speed, add step toward target.

Cue: “Show me your hip first, then let the ball go.” At 8–9 you can name the connection: “This is how your golf swing works.”

Drill 2 — One-Hand Catch at Varying Heights

Partner throws to various heights — low, mid, high, wide. Catcher uses one hand only, alternate hands each set. Progress: catcher does not know which height is coming — reactive tracking. Add movement: catcher takes 2 steps before each catch. Equal time on non-dominant hand — bilateral hand skill transfers to grip pressure and wrist control.

Drill 3 — Wall Rebound Rapid Fire

1.5m from wall. Throw with one hand, catch rebound same hand. Continuous rhythm. Progress: alternate hands each catch. Count consecutive catches, time 30-second streak.

Golf transfer: Reflexive deceleration response — absorbing force quickly and immediately redirecting. Athletic ancestor of wrist and forearm control governing club through and beyond impact. Directly relevant to Post Impact Flyaway.

Drill 4 — Target Throw from Movement (plant-and-throw)

Set target. Run 5m toward it, plant lead foot, throw. Vary approach angle.

Golf transfer: Plant-and-throw = closest athletic movement to lead foot plant and post-up at impact. A child who can run, plant, and throw accurately is building the neurological template for ground force application in golf.

Drill 5 — Two-Ball Juggling Sequence

Start: single ball toss hand to hand in arc. Progress to two balls: throw from dominant when other peaks. Two-ball juggling is the meaningful achievement at 8–9 — do not rush to three balls.

Golf transfer: Juggling ability correlates with superior fine motor control in ball sports.

Ages 10–12 Athletic refinement — golf connections named explicitly ›

Golf connections can be named explicitly now. Rotational patterns are deliberately golf-adjacent.

Drill 1 — Light Med Ball Chest Pass, Catch and Resist

1kg ball only — deceleration quality not load. Partner throws chest pass. Catch with both hands, resist — pull ball into chest slowly over 2 seconds. The 2-second resist is the training stimulus. Gateway drill to full med ball deceleration session at 13+.

Drill 2 — Rotational Throw, Side-On, Both Directions

Side-on to wall or partner. Now explicitly name hip-before-arm sequence. Both sides. Add 1-step approach, then run-up, maintaining hip-first sequence under movement.

Coaching cue: “This is exactly what your downswing does — hips go first, then arms follow. When you throw well, you’re already doing the golf swing.”

Drill 3 — Catch and Freeze (diagnostic drill)

Partner throws rotational chest pass. Receiver catches and immediately freezes for 3 seconds. Correct freeze: arms in front of body, hips not fully open. Arms consistently behind body in freeze = stuck-behind physical pattern.

Most diagnostic drill in the library. A junior who freezes arms-behind at age 10 is showing you the pattern that will appear as Early Rotation in their golf swing.

Drill 4 — Throwing Accuracy Ladder

Targets at 4m, 7m, 10m, 15m. Must hit each in order, 3 attempts per target, before progressing. Non-dominant arm same ladder after dominant. Group competition.

Drill 5 — Partner Resist and Release

Partners hold ball between them at chest height. Both push against ball with moderate resistance. On signal, one player releases — other must immediately decelerate ball without dropping.

Golf transfer: Trains stretch-shortening cycle in arms and shoulders — load eccentrically then absorb. Athletic ancestor of wrist and forearm deceleration that controls club post-impact. Directly relevant to Post Impact Flyaway.

📄 Transition Assessment

The transition assessment determines readiness for fault-specific intervention. It converts the transition decision from a judgment call into a structured assessment across four domains. A junior who meets the minimum standard in all four domains has the foundation fault-specific work needs to land correctly.
When to run: When a junior is being considered for fault-specific intervention — typically at the end of a term where intake screen scores have been rising, or when a fault pattern has been identified. Time required: 20 minutes alongside a regular session. Head coach only.
Domain 1 — Single-Leg Stability

The non-negotiable gateway domain. Every fault-specific physical intervention places demand on a single-leg stable base. The golf downswing is a single-leg stability event on the lead side at the moment of highest load and speed.

Minimum standard — all three must be met:
  • Test 1: 20 seconds both legs, pelvis level, no significant hip drop. Trendelenburg sign on either leg = Domain 1 fail
  • Test 2: 10 seconds dominant leg minimum. Eyes-closed below 50% of eyes-open = visual compensation flag
  • Test 3: Clean 2-second stick landing both sides, knee tracking over foot, pelvis level, no trunk collapse
Intervention when not met:

Balance games library — all games, every session. SL balance progression: eyes open → eyes closed → on folded mat → with upper limb task. Lateral hop and stick progressions. Glute bridge program Weeks 1 and 2 where Test 1 scores 1. Re-screen at 6 weeks.

Domain 2 — Rotational Sequencing

The ability to produce a hip-before-arm movement pattern. Fault-specific drill progressions assume the junior can produce a basic hip-before-arm sequence. Without that reference, drills produce a slower version of the same arm-dominant pattern — they encode compensation rather than correct sequence.

Minimum standard — all three must be met:
  • Rotational throw dominant side: Hip-before-arm sequence visible on at least 2 of 3 throws
  • Catch-and-freeze both sides: Arms in front of body on freeze — both dominant and non-dominant catching sides
  • Slow-motion swing verbal countdown: Clear gap between “3” (delivery) and “4” (through) at 30–40% effort
Intervention when not met:

Overarm throw progression both sides — dominant hip-before-arm development first, non-dominant bilateral work alongside. Catch-and-freeze both sides every throwing session — record arm position, track improvement. Slow-motion swing verbal countdown every session — count aloud, identify merged phases. Re-assess at 4 weeks.

Domain 3 — Postural Endurance

The ability to maintain golf posture for the duration of a fault-specific physical activity. Fault-specific programs involve sustained positions and deliberate pauses — these are higher postural demands than a normal swing, not lower.

Minimum standard — both must be met:
  • Test 4 score 2: 20 seconds stable, pelvis level, spine angle maintained, weight even
  • Address position hold through slow-motion swing: Posture maintained from address through impact at 30% effort — no early extension, spine angle loss, or pelvic tilt change
Intervention when not met:

Golf posture hold in every warm-up — 3 sets of maximum duration, recording time each set. Core activation in tabletop — dead bug progressions. Forward plank progressions. Dynamic posture work — slow hip rotations in golf posture. Re-assess at 4 weeks. If no improvement after two re-assessments, investigate hip flexor tightness or anterior pelvic tilt.

Domain 4 — Body Awareness and Coaching Responsiveness

The ability to sense internal movement qualities and translate that into self-report and movement adjustment. Fault-specific drills are feel-based — they require the junior to access, interpret, and act on internal proprioceptive information. External compliance produces temporary pattern change. Internal learning produces permanent pattern change.

Minimum standard — four observable indicators assessed through session observation:
  • Foot pressure reporting: Can report a specific answer about where pressure was in their feet (not “I don’t know”)
  • Swing feel discrimination: Can identify a difference between a correctly sequenced attempt and an arm-dominant attempt
  • Feel cue responsiveness: A feel cue produces a detectable movement change within 3–5 attempts
  • Left-right body awareness: Aware that one side feels different from the other in at least one observable quality
Intervention when not met:

Eyes-closed balance games are the primary Domain 4 intervention tools. Deliberate feel-reporting practice: after every throwing or balance activity, ask the junior to report one internal sensation. Feel cue practice at very slow speed — introduce one feel cue in a static position before applying it to movement. Re-assess at 6 weeks.

Transition Readiness Profiles

Profile 1 — Full Transition

All four domains met. Proceed to fault-specific intervention. Identify entry point from fault-specific program matched to identified fault.

Profile 2 — Coordination Gap

Domains 1 and 3 met. Domain 2 and/or 4 not met. Coordination sequencing program runs 4–6 weeks before fault-specific drills begin. Physical program (glute bridge, standing stability) begins immediately.

Profile 3 — Physical Gap

Domains 2 and 4 met. Domain 1 and/or 3 not met. Physical foundation program runs 4–6 weeks before fault-specific drills begin. Coordination and rhythm work continues throughout.

Profile 4 — Multiple Gaps

Two or more domains not met across both physical and coordination categories. Extended athletic foundation as primary program. Targeted gap interventions for each domain not met. Re-assess at 6–8 weeks.

🏺 Glute & Hip

The glute and hip programs address the primary physical root of weight transfer, lateral stability, and ground force faults. Physical activation precedes every swing session. Transition assessment clearance required unless stated otherwise.
Glute Bridge 4-Week Program — Age 12+ ›
4 weeks Age 12+ Early Rotation / Stuck Behind Lateral Sway Hanging Back

Root cause: Weak eccentric glute load — no braking capacity. Mechanism: Correct downswing sequence demands eccentric strength — each segment decelerates to transfer energy forward. Juniors lack this braking capacity. The pelvis fires and keeps going because there is no muscular brake. This is a capacity problem disguised as a technique problem. Cue: “Push the floor away with your back heel.”

Week 1 — Activate

Isometric squeeze → bilateral bridge 3×12 with 2s tempo → 20s hold. Establish mind-muscle connection.

Week 2 — Banded Loading

Clam shells 3×15 → banded bilateral bridge 3×12 → bridge with alternating march 3×8/side. Add abduction resistance.

Week 3 — Single-Leg Isolation

SL bridge trail side 3×8 with 3s descent → SL bridge lead side 3×8 → banded SL hold 2×15s. Train hips independently.

Week 4 — Transfer

Bridge to hip hinge cycles 3×10 → standing banded hip load 3×10/side → slow-motion swing with glute focus at 30% effort.

Progression check: Mind-muscle connection → band resistance clean → SL 3s descent controlled → pattern holds in swing.

Exception rule: A junior can begin Weeks 1–2 with Test 1 score of 1 (provided Tests 2 and 3 are not scoring 0). The bridge program itself builds the stability that improves screen scores. Exception does not extend to med ball, speed ramp, or resistance band work.

Post-Bridge Hip 20-Week Hip Development Pathway — Follows Bridge ›
20 weeks (Weeks 5–24) Age 13+ Early Rotation / Stuck Behind Hanging Back Early Extension

Follows completion of the 4-week glute bridge program. Four sequential phases each with clear exit criteria before progression.

Phase 1 — Standing Stability (Weeks 5–8)

SL Romanian deadlift bodyweight (3×8/side, 3s eccentric), lateral band walk in golf posture (3×10/side), standing hip CARs (3×5/side full circle), split squat (3×10/side). Exit: SL RDL 8 clean reps each side, lateral walk 10 steps zero sway, CARs full circle stable.

Phase 2 — Loaded Hip Hinge (Weeks 9–12)

Bilateral RDL with dumbbells progressive overload (4×8, 3s eccentric), SL RDL loaded contralateral (3×6/side), hip thrust loaded (4×8, 1s squeeze), lateral lunge to balance (3×8/side). Load: 4–6kg age 13–14, 8–10kg age 15–16. Exit: Bilateral RDL clean at 25–30kg total, both SL sides within 10%, hip thrust at 50%+ bodyweight.

Phase 3 — Explosive Power (Weeks 13–16)

Broad jump to stick landing (4×5, 2s stick), lateral bound to SL stick (3×4/side), heavy med ball rotational throw max intent (3×6/side). Age 14+ recommended. Exit: Stick landings clean, lateral bound landing stable every rep, swing threshold 85–90%.

Phase 4 — Integration (Weeks 17–20)

Gym drops to 2 sessions/week maintenance. SuperSpeed and Mach 3 now fully appropriate. Maintenance block: banded SL bridge, SL RDL loaded, lateral bound to stick, hip CARs — 2×8 each. Trackman target: Path within ±3° neutral at full effort.

🏗 Core & Posterior Chain

Posterior chain holds the hip hinge. Core stability holds the spine angle within that hinge under rotational load. Both are required simultaneously — run them together from the start, not sequentially.
Posterior Chain Posterior Chain Strength and Posture — 6 Weeks ›
6–8 weeks Age 13+ Early Extension Loss of Posture Reverse Spine Angle S Posture / C Posture

Root cause: Insufficient posterior chain strength and postural endurance — cannot hold the hip hinge under rotational load. Screen indicator: Test 4 score 0 or 1, hip hinge pattern fails. Cue: “Push your hips back — long spine, proud chest.”

Addresses Early Extension and Loss of Posture simultaneously. Where both faults are present, run this program once — it addresses both because they share the same physical deficit.

Golf posture hold 3×20s — record maximum hold time each set → tabletop hold 3×20s → dead bug 3×8/side slow and controlled. Establish basic postural endurance before adding load.

Bodyweight hip hinge 3×12 with 2s hold at bottom — hands slide down thighs, spine angle maintained → RDL with dowel rod on spine 3×10 — rod maintains contact throughout → good morning bodyweight 3×12.

Week 3 — Posterior Chain Load

Dumbbell RDL 3×10 with 3s descent — 4–6kg age 13, 6–8kg age 14–15 → SL RDL bodyweight trail side 3×8 with 3s descent → glute bridge with 3s hold at top 3×12.

Week 4 — Rotational Load Added

Dumbbell RDL progressive load 4×8 → cable pull-through 3×12 — hip hinge pattern under rotational resistance → banded good morning 3×12 with rotation at top.

Week 5 — Single-Leg and Speed

SL RDL with dumbbell 3×8/side — 4–6kg → lateral bound to SL stick with spine angle held 3×5/side → hip hinge to jump 3×6 — hinge then jump, land and hold hinge.

Week 6 — Transfer

Squat to address to swing sequence 3×5 → slow-motion swing with dowel rod on spine → speed ramp from 30% with spine angle monitoring — coach calls “hold” if early extension appears.

Core Stability Core Stability — Integrated alongside Posterior Chain ›
4–6 weeks Age 12+ Early Extension Loss of Posture Reverse Spine Angle

Root cause: Insufficient core stability to maintain spine angle as a stable axis for rotation. Screen indicator: Test 4 score 1 — posture drifts under time demand rather than failing immediately. Cue: “Brace your midline — create a stable centre to rotate around.”

Integration rule: Posterior chain holds the hip hinge. Core stability holds the spine angle within that hinge. Both required. Posterior chain takes priority in Weeks 1–3. Core stability takes priority from Week 4 as the hip hinge pattern stabilises. Not a standalone program — integrated into existing sessions.
Tabletop Progressions

Tabletop hold 20s → alternate arm reach 3×8/side → alternate leg reach 3×8/side → opposite arm and leg bird-dog 3×8/side with 2s hold. Each step adds a stability challenge to the neutral spine.

Dead Bug Progressions

Dead bug alternate arm and leg 3×8/side → dead bug with contralateral arm and leg extension 3×6/side → dead bug with band resistance 3×6/side. Anti-rotation and anti-extension stability specifically.

Plank Progressions

Forearm plank 3×20s → plank with alternate shoulder tap 3×8/side → plank with alternate leg lift 3×8/side → side plank 3×15s/side. Frontal and lateral core stability alongside posterior chain.

Pallof Press — Most Golf-Specific Core Exercise

Cable or band at chest height, perpendicular to body. Press out and hold 2s, return. 3×8/side.

The resistance wants to rotate the body — resisting it trains the stability system that holds the spine angle under rotational load. This is anti-rotation under external load — exactly the demand the golf swing places on the core throughout every phase.

🥀 Thoracic & Shoulder

Thoracic and shoulder programs are daily mobility programs (10–15 min). Mobility gains encode only when immediately followed by swing work in the golf posture context. Never run in isolation. Thoracic extension work before rotation work in every session — extension is the structural prerequisite for rotation.
Thoracic Mobility Thoracic Mobility with Extension Component — 15 min daily ›
6–8 weeks Awareness stage+ (age 10+) Flat Shoulder Plane Reverse Spine Angle Over the Top Loss of Posture Type 1 Reverse Pivot

Root cause: Thoracic rotation restriction and kyphosis — T-spine cannot rotate independently, lead shoulder works over rather than under. Screen indicator: Test 6 score 0 or 1, rounding present at address. Cue: “Open your chest — rotate from your ribcage.” Responds more slowly than hip mobility — allow 6–8 weeks.

Same program addresses: Reverse Spine Angle (thoracic root), Flat Shoulder Plane (primary root), Loss of Posture Type 1, and Reverse Pivot (thoracic root) — run once, addresses all simultaneously.

1 — Thoracic Extension over Foam Roller (always first)

Foam roller perpendicular to spine at mid-back. Lie back, arms crossed on chest. Extend over roller — 5 positions lower to upper thoracic, 5 extensions at each position, hold each 3s.

2 — Cat-Cow with Thoracic Emphasis

On hands and knees. Full spinal flexion to extension — 10 reps slow. On extension phase focus on opening the chest and extending the thoracic region. The lower back is already mobile in most juniors — the thoracic extension is the target.

3 — Thread the Needle

On hands and knees, one arm threads under the body rotating the thoracic spine. Hold 30s each side. 3 sets. Most effective single thoracic rotation mobiliser in the program. The quadruped position stabilises the lumbar spine so the thoracic must do the rotating.

4 — Thoracic Rotation in Golf Posture

Golf address, hands clasped behind head. Rotate shoulders maximally keeping hips still — hold 2s at end range, return. 10 reps each direction. The screen movement used as a training activity — training stimulus is reaching end range and holding.

5 — Open Book

Lie on side, knees bent 90°, arms out in front together. Open top arm toward ceiling rotating thoracic spine — hold 3s at end range. 10 reps each side. Gravity assists end-range access.

6 — Wall Angel

Stand with back against wall, feet 6 inches from wall. Arms bent 90° against wall — slide arms up toward overhead maintaining contact with wall throughout. 10 repetitions. Trains thoracic extension with scapular control — directly addresses rounding pattern at address.

Transfer rule: Immediately after every session run the separation drill with two clubs. New range encodes into the swing pattern only while the tissue is prepared. Mobility in isolation without immediate swing transfer produces range gains on the floor that do not appear in the swing.
Lead Shoulder Lead Shoulder Mobility — 10 min daily ›
4–6 weeks Loading stage+ Flat Shoulder Plane Chicken Wing Post Impact Flyaway

Root cause: Lead shoulder internal rotation and cross-body mobility restriction — lead shoulder cannot work down and under in the backswing. Screen indicator: Cross-body reach restricted, lead shoulder IR restricted, Test 6 borderline or passing. Cue: “Create length across the back of your lead shoulder.”

Key distinction: Thoracic restriction shows on Test 6 with both shoulders affected. Lead shoulder restriction shows with isolated lead shoulder mobility tests while Test 6 may be borderline or passing. This distinction determines whether the program is thoracic mobility, shoulder mobility, or both.
1 — Cross-Body Lead Shoulder Stretch

Lead arm across chest at shoulder height, opposite hand gently holds lead elbow drawing toward opposite shoulder. Hold 45s. 3 sets. Primary stretch for cross-body restriction — directly trains the range the lead shoulder needs to work across and under in the backswing.

2 — Lead Shoulder Internal Rotation Stretch

Lead arm at shoulder height, elbow bent 90°. Use opposite hand or wall to gently rotate lead forearm toward ground. Hold 30s. 3 sets.

3 — Sleeper Stretch

Lie on lead shoulder side, lead arm out at shoulder height, elbow bent 90°. Opposite hand gently presses lead forearm toward floor. Hold 45s. 3 sets. Most effective isolated lead shoulder IR stretch available.

4 — Lead Shoulder CARs

One hand on lead shoulder to isolate joint. Draw the largest circle possible — slow and controlled through full available range. 5 circles each direction. End-range movement is the training stimulus.

5 — Band Pull-Apart with Lead Shoulder Emphasis

Light resistance band at chest height. Pull apart to T position — hold 2s. 15 repetitions. Strengthens posterior shoulder and scapular stabilisers that control lead shoulder position in the backswing — not pure mobility but the strength component that makes mobility gains usable.

Transfer rule: Immediately after every session run the lead shoulder under the chin drill. New range is loaded into the correct movement pattern while tissue is prepared.

Root cause: Trail shoulder external and internal rotation restriction — shoulder cannot maintain connected position through the backswing rotation. Screen indicator: Trail shoulder ER restricted, IR restricted alongside. Cue: “Create length and freedom in the back of your trail shoulder.”

1 — Trail Shoulder Cross-Body Stretch

Trail arm across chest at shoulder height, lead hand gently holds trail elbow drawing toward lead shoulder. Hold 45s. 3 sets. Primary stretch for cross-body and posterior shoulder restriction — directly relevant to the range the trail shoulder needs to maintain connection through the backswing turn.

2 — Trail Shoulder External Rotation Stretch

Trail arm at shoulder height, elbow bent 90°, forearm vertical. Gently rotate forearm backward — use wall or doorframe for support. Hold 30s. 3 sets. Addresses the external rotation restriction that forces the elbow outward when restricted.

One hand on trail shoulder to isolate joint. Largest controlled circle — slow through full available range. 5 circles each direction. Maintains and builds the full joint range rather than targeting a single restriction direction.

Stand in doorframe, trail arm at shoulder height with elbow bent, forearm against frame. Gently rotate body away from trail arm. Hold 45s. 3 sets. Addresses anterior shoulder and pectoral tightness that contributes to the trail arm being forced outward in the backswing.

Scapular Stability Scapular Stability and Posterior Shoulder Strength — 4–6 Weeks ›
4–6 weeks Loading stage+ Post Impact Flyaway Chicken Wing Flying Elbow Flat Shoulder Plane

Root cause: Insufficient posterior shoulder and scapular stabiliser strength — the lead arm lacks the pull capacity to resist the club’s momentum through and past impact, and the trail arm lacks the structural stability to fold and hold correctly through the backswing. Screen indicator: Lead shoulder cross-body mobility borderline or restricted, bilateral forearm deficit present, Post Impact Flyaway or Chicken Wing persisting after forearm program is underway, flat shoulder plane present specifically in the early takeaway. Cue: “Shoulder blades back and down — create a stable base for the arm to work from.”

Key distinction from the shoulder mobility programs: The lead and trail shoulder mobility programs target range of motion — creating space for the arm to move through. This program targets the strength and eccentric capacity of the posterior shoulder chain — the ability to hold and decelerate the arm position under load. A junior can have adequate shoulder mobility but still lack the pull strength to control the club post-impact. Where the mobility programs address restriction, this program addresses capacity. Run them simultaneously where both deficits are confirmed — they are complementary.
Addresses: Post Impact Flyaway (primary — lead arm pull capacity deficit), Chicken Wing (secondary — lead arm structural support through impact), Flying Elbow (tertiary — trail arm scapular stability through backswing), and Flat Shoulder Plane (scapular stability root — when the scapular stabilisers cannot hold the shoulder girdle in its address position through the early takeaway, the trail arm drifts independently from the body rather than the thoracic spine rotating the whole shoulder unit. The shoulder plane flattens as a consequence of arm drift, not restricted rotation. Thoracic mobility work does not address this root — this program does). Running this program once addresses all four simultaneously. Coaching language connecting the gains to each fault differs.
Week 1 — Activate and Anchor

Two exercises run as a pair in every session this week — they address the same scapular stability from opposite directions simultaneously and must not be separated.

Prone I, T, Y, W sequence — bodyweight only, 3×8 of each position with 2s hold at end range. The sequence targets the full posterior shoulder chain progressively: I activates the lower trapezius and rhomboids; T adds mid-trapezius and rear deltoid; Y brings in the lower trapezius specifically; W introduces scapular retraction and depression together. Run all four positions in sequence each session. On each position the shoulder blades must actively move — not the arms independently. Coach observation from behind during the first session confirms this. If the scapulae are not moving the arms are compensating and the exercise is producing nothing useful.

Wall slides 3×10 slow. Stand with back against a wall — feet 6 inches out, lumbar in contact, both arms bent at 90 degrees against the wall. Slide arms overhead maintaining contact at the back of the hands, forearms, and elbows throughout. Return slowly. Wall slides establish the scapular anchor from the anterior side — serratus anterior holding the scapula against the ribcage — before any loaded pull work begins in Week 2. Without this anchor the posterior chain has no stable base to pull from. A junior whose hands lose wall contact on the slide is showing the serratus deficit that would otherwise allow a winging scapula to undermine the pull work in subsequent weeks. Introduce this in Week 1, not Week 4.

Week 2 — Pull Strength with Eccentric Emphasis

With the scapular anchor established in Week 1, loaded pull work now has a stable base to operate from.

Resistance band face pulls 3×12 — elbows at or above shoulder height, scapulae squeezing toward each other at end range, 2s hold. Trains the external rotation and scapular retraction combination that stabilises the lead shoulder through impact.

Band pull-aparts 3×15 — arms at shoulder height, pull to full arm span, hold 2s at end range. Progress from light band to medium band as the end-range hold becomes stable across all 15 reps.

Single-arm cable or band row with slow eccentric — 3×8 per side. The concentric pull is the setup. The 3–4 second eccentric return — allowing the cable or band to slowly pull the arm back to full extension — is the training stimulus. This is the HP principle applied here: the slow controlled return builds the deceleration capacity the arm needs to resist the club’s momentum post-impact. Full arm extension at the end of each eccentric before the next rep — no shortening of the range. The scapular stability established in Week 1 is what allows this eccentric to be performed correctly — a winging scapula during the return phase defeats the purpose entirely.

Week 3 — Load and Push-Pull Balance

Dumbbell bent-over rear deltoid fly 3×12 — hip hinge position, arms at shoulder height, slow and controlled. The golf posture angle is deliberate — the posterior shoulder chain is being loaded in the same spinal position as the swing.

Push-pull balance: introduce push-up or chest press work alongside the pull work. The posterior shoulder chain cannot function as a stable base if the anterior shoulder is significantly stronger. 3×10 push-ups with a controlled 3s descent. Where push-ups are too easy: feet elevated push-ups or dumbbell chest press 3×10.

Ratio principle: for every set of push work complete one set of pull work in the same session. In a junior with a flyaway or chicken wing root, bias slightly toward pull — 2 sets pull for every 1 set push — until the posterior chain catches the anterior.

Prone Y and W with light load — 0.5–1kg plates in hands. Small load, same movement quality as Week 1. The load is sufficient to make the scapular movement a deliberate muscular effort rather than a passive positional hold.

Week 4 — Golf-Posture Integration and Push-Up Plus

Band pull-apart in golf posture 3×12 — junior takes address position, band at chest height, pulls apart and holds 2s at end range. The scapular retraction and depression trained in Weeks 1–3 must now be accessible from the address position — this exercise confirms the transfer.

Single-arm cable or band row in golf posture 3×8 with 3–4s eccentric. Same movement as Week 2 but performed from a hip-hinged address position. The postural demand adds a second layer — posterior chain holds the spine angle while the shoulder chain performs the pull and eccentric return simultaneously. This is the most golf-specific exercise in the program.

Push-up plus 3×10. At the top of a standard push-up position, add a scapular protraction by pushing the floor away further — the upper back rounds slightly and the shoulder blades separate. Then return to the standard top position. This specifically activates serratus anterior in a loaded position. The reason this belongs in Week 4 rather than Week 1 is that meaningful serratus activation requires the posterior shoulder chain to be strong enough to control the scapula during the movement — without that strength the exercise produces a collapse rather than a controlled protraction. Wall slides in Week 1 established the awareness; the push-up plus in Week 4 loads it.

Weeks 5–6 — Transfer and Swing Integration

Pre-activation sequence before every swing session: band pull-aparts 2×10, face pulls 2×10, push-up plus 2×8. Total time 5 minutes. Not a warm-up — a deliberate neural preparation for the stabilisers that prevent flyaway and chicken wing.

Lead arm pull drill (Chicken Wing framework Drill 2) combined with activation: perform the pre-activation sequence then immediately make 10 lead-arm-only swings. The posterior shoulder activation directly precedes the drill that requires it — the timing is the key, not the exercises in isolation.

Extension target drill (Post Impact Flyaway framework Drill 1) at increasing speed. Track the swing speed at which the club fails to reach the extension target — this is the posterior shoulder strength threshold. Rising threshold week on week confirms the program is building the capacity the flyaway framework needs. Where the threshold shows no change after two weeks of the transfer block, return to Week 3 loading for one additional week before retesting.

Progression check: Prone I, T, Y, W with scapular movement confirmed not arm compensation → wall slides with full contact maintained throughout the arc → band face pull and pull-apart with 2s hold at end range across all reps → single-arm row eccentric 3–4s controlled to full extension → all pull exercises transferable to golf posture position → push-up plus producing controlled protraction not collapse → pre-activation sequence before swing sessions → extension target drill threshold rising on Trackman.

Age gate note: The eccentric loading demand of the single-arm row (Week 2) and the golf-posture row (Week 4) are appropriate from Loading stage minimum — age 13 with transition assessment clearance. The prone I, T, Y, W sequence and wall slides are appropriate from Awareness stage (age 10+) as activation work without the eccentric loading component. Push-up progression appropriate from age 10+ where the junior can maintain a neutral spine through the full range — confirm Test 4 before introducing.

Grip Pressure Grip Pressure Rebalancing — 2–4 Weeks ›
2–4 weeks Awareness stage+ Over the Top Flying Elbow Casting / Scooping

Root cause: Trail hand grip pressure excess driving trail shoulder dominance and trail arm rigidity. Screen indicator: Trail hand significantly tighter than lead hand on grip pressure test. Addresses Over the Top and Flying Elbow simultaneously. Trail hand grip tension produces over the top at the shoulder level (trail shoulder fires early) and flying elbow at the arm level (rigidity prevents correct trail arm folding). Run it once — addresses both through the same physical mechanism.
Step 1 — Establish Baseline

Ask junior to rate lead and trail hand pressure separately on 1–10 during a normal grip. Most juniors with this root rate trail hand 7–9 and lead hand 4–6. Target relationship: lead hand equal to or 1–2 points above trail hand.

Step 2 — Calibration Drills

Blindfold grip pressure calibration — Levels 2 and 4 specifically. Level 2 trains lead hand pressure awareness. Level 4 trains differential pressure simultaneously. Not light overall grip — firm lead hand, guided trail hand.

Step 3 — Pre-Shot Routine Integration

Every swing during the program period begins with a deliberate grip pressure check. Set lead hand pressure first, then place trail hand at deliberately lighter pressure. Interrupts the automatic trail-hand-first gripping pattern.

Step 4 — Transition Monitoring

After each swing, junior self-reports whether trail hand felt light or tight in the transition. Tight = trail shoulder trigger present. Light = sequencing available. Track frequency of “light” reports across sessions.

Progress: Conscious check every swing → check every third swing → internal monitoring only → periodic check as maintenance.

Speed Ramp Speed Ramp Protocol — 4-Week Sequencing Under Load ›
4 weeks Age 11+ Early Rotation / Stuck Behind All speed-induced faults

Neurological principle: Patterns encode at the speed they are practised. Speed ramping progressively loads the pattern until the ceiling rises. Track the threshold weekly — the speed at which the pattern breaks. Threshold rise is the measurable outcome, not ball flight improvement.

Week 1 — Establish (20–50% effort, 7-iron only, 30 swings/session)

1A: Verbal countdown 8 reps at 20% — count “1-2-3-4” out loud, gap between 3 and 4 = correct, merged = rotation beating arms. 1B: Pause at top 8 reps at 35% — 2s pause, initiate with pressure shift not rotation. 1C: First ball contact 14 reps at 50% max — self-report after each swing.

Week 2 — Challenge (50–70% effort, ball every rep)

2A: Threshold finder — escalate 40/50/60/70%, first level where 2/3 break = threshold. 2B: Threshold training block 12 swings at threshold — add 5% if 3 consecutive hold. 2C: 5-iron introduction 8 swings at 50% only.

Week 3 — Load (65–85% effort, multiple clubs, variable targets)

3A: Variable club rotation 18 swings, alternate 7/5-iron every 3 at 75%. 3B: Target pressure swings 10 swings at 85% — announce target before each. 3C: Corridor swings 12 swings at 75% — external focus, no sequencing cues, coach observes silently.

Week 4 — Integrate (full effort available, game simulation)

4A: Full intent block 12 swings — no constraints, coach observes, compare to baseline Trackman. 4B: Course simulation 9 holes from range. 4C: Threshold audit — repeat Week 2 threshold finder, compare, plan next block.

Threshold interpretation: No change → motor learning issue, extend Weeks 1–2. +0–5% → physical ceiling limiting, return to glute/med ball program. +5–10% → good progress, repeat Weeks 3–4 with longer clubs. +10–15% → strong response, add driver and speed tools.

Balance and proprioception programs are appropriate at all ages and do not require transition assessment clearance. They are the primary physical intervention at Foundation stage and continue as maintenance for all juniors throughout the program.
Trail Side Balance Trail Side Balance and Proprioceptive Development — 6–8 Weeks ›

Screen indicator: Trail side SL balance significantly below lead side, trail side hip drop greater than lead side. No transition assessment required. Appropriate from Foundation stage using the games library. Where the balance asymmetry is the primary root, this program alone may resolve the fault. If balance scores improve but fault persists, add the trail hip loading program (Section 1 above).
Trail Side Balance Emphasis

Run all balance progressions with deliberate trail side emphasis — 60% of balance work on trail side, 40% lead side for the program period. All progressions trail side first: eyes open → eyes closed → on folded mat → with upper limb task.

Trail Side Flamingo Freeze Benchmark

Record trail side and lead side flamingo freeze times every session. Primary progress indicator: target trail side within 20% of lead side by end of program. If trail side is more than 50% below lead side at start — proprioceptive deficit is significant, this is the priority intervention before any swing drill work.

Trail Side Eyes-Closed Single-Leg

Pure proprioceptive system training — not just balance. Eyes-closed SL on trail side trains the proprioceptive independence that allows the trail side to be a confident loading option. Target: 10 seconds trail side eyes closed by end of program period.

Trail Side Sumo Statue

Partner pushing game on trail leg. Absorbing external force while maintaining trail-side SL balance trains reactive stability — the same system that holds the trail-side load during the backswing under the rotational force of the shoulder turn.

Trail Side Balance and Throw (most golf-specific)

Stand on trail leg, throw at target with lead arm. The combined demand of trail-side balance and lead-arm throwing directly mirrors the backswing demand — trail side loaded, upper body rotating. Most golf-specific balance activity in the library for reverse pivot correction.

Spatial Awareness Spatial Awareness Development — Integrated Program ›
4–8 weeks Awareness stage+ (age 10+) Flying Elbow All positional faults

Spatial awareness in golf operates at two levels. Macro: where is the target, what is the line, what is the required trajectory. Micro: where is the club face, where is the trail elbow, where is the lead hip. Poor micro spatial awareness produces the compensating movements that appear as swing faults — not because the junior doesn’t understand correct positions, but because they cannot accurately sense where their body actually is.

Connection to Domain 4: Spatial awareness development is a Domain 4 body awareness intervention. Run these activities for 3–4 weeks before introducing feel-based fault-specific drills — the junior can then feel what the drills are building rather than executing them as external compliance tasks.
Eyes-Closed Top Position Hold

Junior makes a backswing and holds top position — eyes closed throughout. Coach observes trail elbow. Junior self-reports where the elbow is pointing — toward ground, outward, or upward. Coach confirms without moving the junior. 5 reps each session. Track self-report accuracy across sessions — rising accuracy confirms spatial awareness developing.

Trail Elbow Tap Drill

Coach taps the trail elbow at the top of backswing — junior must report tap location and elbow direction without looking. The physical tap provides a proprioceptive reference point that anchors the internal spatial map. Progress: tap at top → tap at halfway back → tap at address.

Slow Backswing with Position Reporting

Junior makes a slow backswing counting aloud — “1 address... 2 halfway... 3 top.” At each count reports trail elbow direction before coach confirms. Multi-point reporting builds a continuous internal spatial map rather than a single top-of-backswing snapshot.

Mirror Backswing Work

Slow backswings in front of a mirror — first watching the trail elbow in mirror, then same backswing eyes closed, then opening eyes to confirm. Alternating visual confirmation and proprioceptive monitoring builds the internal map by connecting what is seen to what is felt.

Blindfold Grip Pressure Calibration

Eyes closed. Coach calls a grip pressure number (1 = barely holding, 10 = maximum squeeze). Junior adjusts and holds. Junior self-reports pressure number after each adjustment — compare to coach observation. Grip pressure is the most direct kinaesthetic variable in golf contact quality.

Shadow Swing Observation

Swing with sun behind you — observe trail elbow position in shadow at top of backswing. Bridges between external visual feedback and internal proprioceptive monitoring. Loss of shadow position reveals spatial awareness gaps: loss at top → trail elbow awareness gap; loss at transition → synchronisation gap; loss post-impact → through-swing spatial deficit.
